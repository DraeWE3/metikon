Twenty Years in the Dark

Nsofang is a farming village in Cross River State. Its residents grow cocoa, plantain, and other staple crops. It is exactly the kind of community that feeds Nigeria. And for more than two decades, it did so without electricity.

No grid connection. No reliable power of any kind. Households and small businesses operated on kerosene lamps and the occasional petrol generator. Economic activity was constrained to daylight hours. Young people left for cities. Housing development stalled. The village was, by any measurable standard, energy-excluded.

In September 2024, that changed.

The System

Metikon Engineering Limited designed, installed, and commissioned a hybrid solar mini-grid for Nsofang Village under the Rural Electrification Agency (REA) Nigeria Electrification Project (NEP) — a Federal Government initiative, funded by the World Bank and African Development Bank, that deploys private sector-led renewable energy solutions to unserved and underserved communities across Nigeria.

"The system specification: 117 kWp solar photovoltaic array · 445 kWh battery energy storage system · 100 kVA inverter · 100 kVA diesel generator for backup · Full distribution network serving households and businesses across the village."

The architecture is a solar-first hybrid: the PV array and battery storage carry the primary load, with the backup generator available for extended periods of low irradiance or exceptional demand. This configuration is designed for reliability in a community that cannot afford intermittent supply — not after waiting 20 years for any supply at all.

What Happened Next

The system has been running continuously since September 2024 — sustainable, uninterrupted power delivery for over 18 months and counting. The mini-grid now serves close to 400 connections and growing, impacting an estimated 1,500 lives across the village.

The impact on Nsofang has been immediate and measurable. Economic activity has grown astronomically. Farmers who previously had no way to process, store, or add value to their cocoa and plantain harvests after dark now operate on extended timelines. Small businesses — shops, food processing, mobile phone charging services — have emerged or expanded. The commercial life of the village is no longer capped by sunset.

But the most telling indicator is not commercial output. It is movement. People are coming back. Nsofang is experiencing remigration — former residents returning to the village — and new housing development is underway. When a community starts building again, it is making a bet on its own future. That bet is underwritten by reliable power.

The REA NEP Programme

The Nigeria Electrification Project is the Federal Government's flagship initiative for closing the energy access gap. It is private sector-driven, deploying solar hybrid mini-grids, standalone solar home systems, and productive use appliances to communities that the national grid has not reached and may not reach for decades. The programme has already deployed over 100 mini-grids across Nigeria, impacting hundreds of thousands of people.

"Electrification does not simply add a utility to a community. It changes the decision calculus for every household — whether to stay, whether to return, whether to invest. Nsofang is proof that when you deliver reliable power to an agrarian community, the economic response is not incremental. It is transformative."

Metikon Engineering Limited operates within this programme as a qualified developer and installer. Nsofang is one of the sites we have delivered under the NEP, and we are currently in the pipeline for additional deployments across multiple states as the programme expands into its next phase.

The REA NEP model works because it aligns incentives: the government provides performance-based grants that close the viability gap, private sector developers bring technical execution and operational discipline, and communities receive power systems designed for long-term sustainability rather than short-term political visibility. For Metikon, this is a natural extension of what we do — engineering energy systems that work, in places where working power supply has the highest marginal impact.

What This Means for Metikon

Nsofang sits alongside Sterling Towers in our project portfolio, but it represents a different facet of the same capability. Sterling Towers is BIPV at Africa's largest scale on a commercial high-rise. Nsofang is a community-scale hybrid mini-grid in a remote agrarian village. Both required the same underlying discipline: power systems engineering, energy storage integration, distribution design, and commissioning to operational standards.

The range is the point. Metikon is not a company that does one type of project at one scale. We are a grid technology and energy systems company. We engineer power — for buildings, for communities, for institutions — wherever the gap between energy supply and economic potential is widest.

Nsofang had no power for 20 years. It now has a system that has run without interruption since the day we switched it on. That is the standard we build to.
